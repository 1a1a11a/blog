For three weeks, I went outside each morning without deciding where the walk would end. The only rule was that I could not optimize the route.

At first, this felt inefficient. I repeated streets. I reached dead ends. I watched buses pass that would have carried me farther in less time.

## The scale of a neighborhood

After a few days, distance began to change. A place was no longer fifteen minutes away. It was past the bakery vent, beyond the sycamore with the low branch, and around the corner where someone practiced scales on an open window.

These details had always been there. Speed had simply made them irrelevant.

> Attention does not make the world more interesting. It reveals how interesting the world already is.

Walking slowly did not produce a revelation. It produced something better: a collection of ordinary evidence that I was present for my own days.
