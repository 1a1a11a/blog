Taste is a practice

Good judgment isn't mysterious. It is built through attention, comparison, and the courage to revise.

March 3, 2026

We often speak about taste as if it were a private inheritance. Some people have it; others do not. This makes taste sound mysterious and conveniently excuses us from improving it.

Taste is less like a possession and more like a practice.

Build a vocabulary for difference

The work begins with comparison. Put two sentences, chairs, interfaces, or photographs beside each other. Ask what each one is trying to do. Notice where your attention moves and where it stalls.

Naming the difference matters. “I like this” is a reaction. “This works because the hierarchy makes the choice obvious” is a reusable observation.

Revise beyond correctness

The first version can be functional and still lack conviction. Revision is where we move past whether something works and ask whether it says what we mean.

Taste grows whenever we refuse to let “good enough” end the conversation too early.

Over time, these comparisons become instinct. The instinct feels immediate, but it is built from accumulated attention. Good judgment is memory moving quickly.
